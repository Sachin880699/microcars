Peel P50

The Peel P50 is one of the smallest production cars ever made, produced by the Peel Engineering Company on the Isle of Man in the early 1960s. Built as an ultra-compact city runabout, the P50 was designed to be minimal, lightweight and inexpensive—perfect for short urban journeys and tight parking spaces.

Designed as a single-seat microcar, the Peel P50 became famous for its tiny footprint and quirky character. Early models used a small-displacement engine and simple mechanicals, prioritizing economy and maneuverability over speed or comfort.

Compact Practicality

Despite its size, the P50 offered basic practicality: an enclosed cabin, a single door on the left, and just enough space for a driver and a small amount of luggage. Its featherweight construction made it exceptionally easy to move and park—stories of owners carrying the P50 up stairs are part of its folklore.

Mechanical Layout

Original Peel P50s were powered by a tiny petrol engine (around 49 cc) with modest power output, sufficient for city speeds. The drivetrain was simple and reliable for the era, reflecting the P50’s role as an economical urban transport solution.
